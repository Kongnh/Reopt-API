# CEBA DPPA Buyer Decision Journey

## Detailed Slide-by-Slide Facilitator Narrative

**Session:** Understanding Vietnam's DPPA Mechanisms and Pricing Considerations  
**Audience:** Factory-owner representatives in global-brand supply chains  
**Presenter:** Cong Nguyen, Vietnam Clean Energy Manager, Allotrope Partners  
**Deck:** `CEBA_DPPA_Buyer_Decision_Journey_2026.pptx`  
**Source and regulatory basis:** As of June 12, 2026

## Facilitation Approach

This is a buyer-decision training session, not a legal or regulatory lecture.
The central question is not simply, "How does a Vietnam DPPA work?" The central
question is, "What evidence does a factory buyer need before it can decide
whether a DPPA proposal is commercially acceptable?"

Use the six-decision journey throughout the session:

1. Establish the buyer's EVN business-as-usual baseline.
2. Decode every line of the DPPA bill.
3. Test the commercial offer under multiple scenarios.
4. Check buyer, seller, and lender feasibility gates.
5. Stress-test the deal using the anonymized Factory A evidence.
6. Convert the findings into negotiation questions.

Maintain three distinctions in all explanations:

- **Regulatory mechanics:** requirements and calculation structures established
  by Decree 57 and related primary sources.
- **Training or model assumptions:** numerical values used to demonstrate or
  model the mechanism, but not confirmed as current regulated values.
- **Negotiated commercial terms:** strike, escalation, contract volume, tenor,
  termination, and risk allocation.

## Slide 1 - Understanding Vietnam's DPPA Mechanisms and Pricing Considerations

**Objective:** Open the session as a practical buyer-decision journey.

**Detailed narrative:**

Welcome participants and introduce the session as a commercial decision
workshop for factory representatives. Explain that a virtual DPPA can help a
factory pursue clean-energy and supply-chain objectives, but it also introduces
new settlement lines, market-price exposure, volume risk, and financing
constraints. The session will therefore avoid treating a DPPA as a single
renewable-energy price.

Introduce the six stages shown on the slide: baseline, bill, offer, gates,
stress test, and checklist. Tell participants that every technical concept will
be connected to a buyer action. By the end, they should be able to ask a
developer for the evidence needed to evaluate an offer and identify which
terms require negotiation.

**Key emphasis:** This is not a promise that DPPAs automatically reduce cost.

**Transition:** Start with why different tests can produce very different
conclusions about the same offer.

---

## Slide 2 - The same DPPA offer can look cheap, expensive, or unfinanceable depending on the test you run

**Objective:** Establish the three tests that control the buyer's conclusion.

**Detailed narrative:**

Explain that a proposal can appear attractive when compared only with an
expected future electricity price, but unattractive when compared with the
factory's actual EVN bill. It can also appear acceptable to the buyer while
failing the developer's investment threshold or the lender's debt-service
requirements.

Walk through the three tests. First, the **EVN BAU test** asks what the factory
would pay if it did nothing. Second, the **DPPA bill test** asks what all
settlement lines total after market energy, fees, residual EVN purchases, and
the bilateral CfD are included. Third, the **three-gate test** asks whether the
buyer, seller, and lender can all accept the same commercial structure.

**Audience prompt:** "Which test is the offer currently being judged against?"

**Key emphasis:** A headline strike price is not enough to answer any of these
three tests.

**Transition:** Introduce the six-decision sequence used to organize diligence.

---

## Slide 3 - Six decisions turn a DPPA proposal into a negotiable term sheet

**Objective:** Give participants a repeatable diligence framework.

**Detailed narrative:**

Present the six stages as a checklist that can be reused for any developer
proposal. The first two stages establish facts: the buyer's baseline and the
actual bill mechanics. The next two stages test the proposed commercial offer
and whether it can support the project financing. The final stages use
scenario evidence to identify the terms and risks that must change.

Explain that formulas will appear only where they change a buyer decision. The
goal is not for every participant to become a settlement specialist. The goal
is for participants to recognize when a developer has not provided enough
data, when a model relies on assumptions rather than current published values,
and when a term sheet transfers risk to the factory.

**Buyer action:** Use this six-stage sequence to structure internal review and
developer diligence.

**Transition:** Begin Decision 1 by defining the correct cost benchmark.

## Slide 4 - Your DPPA benchmark is the EVN bill you would otherwise keep paying

**Objective:** Establish the factory's real business-as-usual benchmark.

**Detailed narrative:**

Explain that the buyer cannot assess savings until it understands the bill it
would continue paying without the DPPA. The relevant baseline depends on the
factory's production voltage, hourly consumption pattern, current retail
tariff, and the applied time-of-use periods.

Use the table to show that off-peak, normal, and peak prices differ by voltage
band. A developer using a generic average tariff may materially misstate buyer
value. The buyer should therefore provide at least 12 monthly EVN bills and
interval meter data, and should confirm the delivery voltage used in the
model.

State that the displayed rates come from EVN's retail tariff under Decision
1279/QD-BCT. The legacy time-of-use bands remained applied as of the latest
MOIT implementation status located for this deck.

**Buyer action:** Request the voltage band, 12 monthly bills, and interval data.

**Transition:** After establishing the baseline, confirm that the meter is
eligible and that the loss-factor treatment is understood.

## Slide 5 - Before pricing, confirm whether the regulation can settle your meter

**Objective:** Prevent pricing discussions before eligibility and loss factors
are confirmed.

**Detailed narrative:**

Explain that the first eligibility question is the buyer's connection voltage.
For the national-grid DPPA mechanism, Decree 57 identifies buyers connected at
22 kV or above. The buyer should confirm the actual delivery point and not rely
only on a corporate-level description of the facility.

Then explain the importance of the annual distribution loss factor, Kpp. Kpp
affects the relationship between generator-metered renewable output and the
quantity treated as delivered for settlement. Article 16 defines an annual
calculation using prior loss data, but this deck did not confirm a current
numerical primary-source publication. Numerical Kpp values shown later are
therefore examples or model assumptions.

**Audience prompt:** "What voltage is our delivery point, and which published
loss factor will settle our bill?"

**Key caveat:** Do not present example Kpp values as current official values.

**Transition:** Show how the physical grid transaction and bilateral financial
contract coexist.

---

## Slide 6 - A virtual DPPA changes the cash flows, not the physical electrons serving the factory

**Objective:** Separate physical electricity delivery from financial settlement.

**Detailed narrative:**

Explain the transaction from left to right. The renewable generator sells
electricity into the market. The market and EVN meter the transaction, deliver
electricity through the national grid, and bill the factory. The factory
continues to receive physical electricity through the grid and continues to
have an EVN-related bill.

The bilateral two-way CfD sits on top of this grid transaction. It creates a
separate financial settlement between the generator and buyer based on the
relationship between the negotiated strike and the market price. The CfD does
not cause specific physical electrons to travel directly from the generator to
the factory.

**Buyer action:** Keep EVN/grid bill cash flows separate from bilateral CfD
cash flows in the model and term sheet.

**Transition:** Break the buyer's monthly cost into its five component lines.

---

## Slide 7 - The buyer's monthly DPPA cost is five lines, not one price

**Objective:** Give participants the core settlement mental model.

**Detailed narrative:**

Walk through the five lines in sequence. First is market energy for the matched
settlement quantity at the delivered market price. Second is the DPPA service
charge. Third is the balancing-difference component. Fourth is the residual
EVN purchase for consumption not covered by the matched DPPA quantity. Fifth
is the bilateral CfD settlement between buyer and generator.

Explain that the strike price directly determines only the CfD line. It does
not automatically include all grid-related charges, losses, residual retail
purchases, or other settlement components. A proposal that advertises only a
strike can therefore hide a large part of the buyer's total cost.

The displayed 360 VND/kWh service value and 163.3 VND/kWh balancing value come
from NSMO training/model evidence. Current regulated publications confirming
these exact amounts were not located.

**Audience prompt:** "Which bill lines stay outside the negotiated strike?"

**Transition:** Explain how hourly consumption caps settlement volume.

---

## Slide 8 - Settlement is capped by what your factory actually consumes

**Objective:** Explain why hourly matching matters more than annual percentages.

**Detailed narrative:**

Describe the volume bridge. The generator produces a metered renewable
quantity. That quantity is adjusted for the applicable loss factor to estimate
delivered volume. The buyer's settlement quantity is then capped by the lower
of the factory's hourly load and the adjusted renewable volume. The CfD
quantity is further limited by the contracted amount.

This protects the buyer from settling renewable volume it did not consume in
that hour. However, it also means that a developer's claim that renewable
generation equals a high percentage of annual consumption does not prove that
the same percentage creates financial settlement value. Timing and shape
matter.

**Buyer action:** Require hourly matching against actual meter data, not only
annual energy totals.

**Transition:** Show why apparently modest grid-related adders can materially
change the offer.

---

## Slide 9 - Grid-related charges can consume much of the apparent CfD value

**Objective:** Demonstrate the potential significance of non-strike charges.

**Detailed narrative:**

Use the three figures to show the commercial effect of the training/model
assumptions. A 360 VND/kWh service component plus a 163.3 VND/kWh balancing
component produces a combined 523.3 VND/kWh adder before considering other
buyer costs. This can be large relative to the difference between the strike
and market price.

Explain that these figures are useful for understanding how fixed or
formula-based adders affect an offer. They must not be treated as confirmed
current regulated fees. Before using them in approval materials, the buyer
should request the current published source, effective period, and calculation
basis.

**Audience prompt:** "Which current published fee and loss-factor values are
being used in this offer?"

**Transition:** Apply the concepts to a worked NSMO settlement example.

## Slide 10 - The NSMO example shows why the bill must be reconciled line by line

**Objective:** Set up a reproducible worked settlement example.

**Detailed narrative:**

Explain that the purpose of the example is not to present current fee levels.
Its purpose is to show the input ledger needed to reproduce a settlement.
Review the buyer's monthly consumption, generator spot volume, FMP, strike,
price/loss factors, and service/balancing assumptions.

Point out why each input matters. Consumption caps buyer settlement. Generator
spot volume supports generator revenue. The difference between strike and FMP
determines CfD direction. Loss and price factors change the delivered market
energy calculation. Service and balancing values create additional buyer
charges.

**Buyer action:** Ask the developer to provide an equivalent input ledger
before presenting savings results.

**Key caveat:** This is an NSMO training example, not confirmation of current
fee values.

**Transition:** Reconcile the inputs into the official monthly totals.

**Objective:** Make reproducibility a procurement control.

**Detailed narrative:**

Walk through the calculated monthly amounts. Market energy, C_DN, is
7,446,297,600 VND. The training service component is 2,160,000,000 VND. The
training balancing component is 979,800,000 VND. The CfD settlement is
600,000,000 VND because the strike exceeds FMP in this example.

Together, the buyer total reaches approximately 11.186 billion VND per month.
The generator total revenue is approximately 7.858 billion VND. Explain that
these totals become understandable only when quantity definitions, market
prices, loss factors, fees, and the CfD are all visible.

The procurement lesson is that the buyer should be able to independently
reproduce a monthly settlement total. A model that cannot be reconciled should
not be used as the basis for approval.

**Audience prompt:** "Can we reproduce your monthly settlement total from the
inputs?"

**Transition:** Convert the bill explanation into a concrete data request.

## Slide 12 - After decoding the bill, the first negotiation is really about evidence

**Objective:** Define the minimum buyer data pack.

**Detailed narrative:**

Explain that the buyer should request evidence before negotiating a headline
price. The minimum data pack includes interval load, the confirmed voltage
band, the current retail tariff, the published loss factor or calculation
basis, the source for service and balancing components, and the relevant FMP
and CFMP series.

Each item controls a different part of the buyer's result. Interval load
controls matching. Voltage affects eligibility and tariff treatment. Tariffs
define BAU. Loss factors and fees affect settlement. Market-price series
control the market-energy and CfD outcomes.

**Buyer action:** Request the full data pack before discussing claimed savings.

**Key emphasis:** Missing evidence is not a minor modeling issue; it is a
commercial-risk issue.

**Transition:** Move from decoding the bill to testing the commercial offer.

---

## Slide 13 - A two-way CfD buys price certainty; it does not guarantee savings

**Objective:** Explain CfD direction without overloading participants with
formula detail.

**Detailed narrative:**

Explain the two possible settlement directions. When FMP is below the strike,
the difference is positive and the buyer pays the generator on the matched CfD
volume. When FMP is above the strike, the difference is negative and the
generator pays the buyer, offsetting part of the buyer's market-related
exposure.

This structure can reduce price uncertainty for the generator and buyer, but it
does not guarantee savings against the factory's EVN BAU bill. Savings depend
on the strike, market-price path, matching volume, tariff path, fees, and
contract terms.

**Audience prompt:** "What FMP scenarios make us pay, and what scenarios make
us receive?"

**Buyer action:** Model a range of market-price scenarios instead of relying
on one expected path.

**Transition:** Show how strike escalation can improve financing while
worsening buyer economics.

---

## Slide 14 - Escalation can help bankability while making Year 1 harder for the buyer

**Objective:** Highlight the trade-off created by strike escalation.

**Detailed narrative:**

Compare a flat strike with an escalating strike. A flat strike can be easier
for the buyer to budget and may limit future cost growth, but it can weaken the
developer's long-term revenue and financing case. An escalating strike can
support project bankability by growing revenue over time, but it may increase
the buyer's premium against BAU and create approval difficulty.

The displayed 2,000 VND/kWh starting strike and 4% annual escalation are
Factory A model assumptions. They are negotiated inputs, not regulated prices
or confirmed market standards.

**Buyer action:** Ask for flat, escalating, and indexed strike cases evaluated
against the same BAU assumptions.

**Key emphasis:** Do not review escalation separately from the starting strike
and BAU escalation.

**Transition:** Explain why annual contract volume must be tested hour by hour.

---

## Slide 15 - Over-contracting beyond hourly consumption does not create more buyer settlement value

**Objective:** Connect hourly matching to contract-volume negotiation.

**Detailed narrative:**

Use the load-versus-solar shape to show that only hourly overlap creates buyer
settlement value. Renewable output that occurs when factory consumption is low
does not automatically increase the buyer's matched CfD quantity. A high
annual contract percentage can therefore overstate useful volume and create
misleading savings expectations.

Explain that the buyer should test multiple contract-volume levels using its
actual 8,760-hour load profile. This helps identify where additional contracted
volume stops creating buyer value and where downside-load risk becomes
material.

**Buyer action:** Request an 8,760-hour volume sweep using actual meter data.

**Key emphasis:** Volume is a shape and timing question, not only an annual
percentage.

**Transition:** Introduce why the developer and lender need a sufficiently
strong strike.

---

## Slide 16 - Your strike becomes the developer's bankable revenue line

**Objective:** Explain the developer and lender perspective without losing the
buyer lens.

**Detailed narrative:**

Explain that the generator receives spot-market revenue and a CfD settlement
that stabilizes part of the revenue around the negotiated strike. This
stabilized revenue is important to the developer's return and the lender's
confidence that debt service can be paid.

The lender is not financing the buyer's sustainability target. It is financing
a project cash-flow stream. The lender therefore examines which revenue is
contracted, which is exposed to spot-market volatility, and whether annual
cash flow provides enough cushion above debt service.

**Audience prompt:** "Which revenue line supports debt service, and which line
is exposed to spot price?"

**Key emphasis:** A lower strike benefits the buyer only if the resulting
project remains financeable.

**Transition:** Define the three gates that must pass together.

---

## Slide 17 - A deal only works when buyer, seller, and lender gates all pass at once

**Objective:** Establish the balanced-deal decision rule.

**Detailed narrative:**

Define each gate. The buyer gate asks whether the DPPA creates acceptable
savings or value versus BAU over the relevant horizons. The seller gate asks
whether equity returns meet the investor's threshold. The lender gate asks
whether minimum annual debt-service coverage remains above the required floor.

Explain why these gates can conflict. A low strike may make the buyer positive
but reduce project cash flow until lender DSCR fails. A higher strike may
create an attractive seller return and strong DSCR while making the buyer
negative versus BAU.

Emphasize minimum DSCR rather than average DSCR because a single weak year can
create financing difficulty.

**Buyer action:** Ask for a gate table, not only a headline strike.

**Transition:** Show the lifecycle assumptions that can shift the gate results.

---

## Slide 18 - Lifecycle items move the bankable strike even when they are absent from the term sheet

**Objective:** Expand diligence beyond price and volume.

**Detailed narrative:**

Explain how four lifecycle items affect project economics. PV degradation can
reduce renewable output and matched volume over time. A battery replacement
can create a large one-year cash outflow and reduce minimum DSCR. Tax holidays,
carryforward treatment, and the timing of taxable income affect seller returns.
Foreign-exchange assumptions affect any long-term USD-facing interpretation of
VND cash flows.

The Factory A model holds FX at 25,000 VND/USD across the model horizon. This
is a simplifying model assumption and requires sensitivity before
investor-facing use.

**Buyer action:** Clarify who bears replacement, degradation, tax, and FX risk
before agreeing to tenor and pricing.

**Transition:** Apply the full decision framework to the anonymized factory
case.

---

## Slide 19 - The same factory that looked onsite-ready can still struggle offsite

**Objective:** Introduce the Factory A virtual-DPPA stress test.

**Detailed narrative:**

Explain that Cases 5 and 6 use the same anonymized factory and the same virtual
DPPA commercial frame. Both use a grid-connected bilateral CfD overlay under
Decree 57, a modeled starting strike of 2,000 VND/kWh, and modeled annual
escalation of 4% over 25 years. The main difference is project sizing,
especially the battery.

Frame this as a continuation of earlier onsite analysis while ensuring the
audience can follow it independently. The important question is whether the
offer passes the buyer, seller, and lender gates using the factory's actual
meter and modeled project economics.

**Audience prompt:** "Does this offer pass our gates on our own meter, not a
reference load?"

**Key caveat:** The strike and escalation are negotiated model inputs, not
regulated terms.

**Transition:** Compare the two sizing outcomes.

---

## Slide 20 - Right-sizing fixes lender comfort, but not buyer value at the offered strike

**Objective:** Show the difference between project bankability and buyer value.

**Detailed narrative:**

Compare Case 5 and Case 6. Case 5 combines solar with a large battery. It
produces a 16.9% seller equity IRR and USD 1.52 million NPV, but minimum DSCR
is only 1.14x, below the modeled 1.20x lender gate. Buyer lifetime savings are
negative 9.3%.

Case 6 uses the same solar capacity with a minimum battery. Lower capital cost
raises seller equity IRR to 26.9%, NPV to USD 2.54 million, and minimum DSCR to
1.50x. The project becomes more financeable, but buyer lifetime savings worsen
to negative 14.4% at the modeled 2,000 VND/kWh strike.

**Buyer action:** Require both project-sizing economics and buyer cost impacts.

**Key lesson:** Right-sizing can solve a lender problem without solving the
buyer's value problem.

**Transition:** Focus directly on the Case 6 three-gate result.

---

## Slide 21 - At 2,000 VND/kWh, the leaner project is financeable but still buyer-negative

**Objective:** Demonstrate that seller, lender, and buyer conclusions can
diverge.

**Detailed narrative:**

Use the three metrics to make the divergence explicit. The seller sees a 26.9%
equity IRR. The lender sees a 1.50x minimum DSCR. Both outcomes look strong
under the tested assumptions. The buyer, however, sees negative 14.4%
lifetime savings versus BAU.

Explain that this is why buyer representatives should not accept "bankable" as
evidence that the offer creates buyer value. Bankability answers whether the
project can attract capital and repay debt. It does not answer whether the
factory pays less or receives sufficient strategic value for the premium.

**Audience prompt:** "Where does buyer value appear, and does the lender still
pass at that strike?"

**Key caveat:** This is the modeled Case 6 result, not a universal Vietnam
market conclusion.

**Transition:** Show what happened when strike and volume were swept.

---

## Slide 22 - Pricing alone did not balance the modeled Case 6 deal under the tested assumptions

**Objective:** Present the central case-study conclusion with precise scope.

**Detailed narrative:**

Explain that 56 Case 6 strike-and-volume scenarios were tested across the main
and lower-strike sweeps. Zero of those 56 tested scenarios passed the buyer,
seller, and lender gates together.

The closest buyer-positive tested term used a 1,300 VND/kWh strike and 70%
volume. It produced positive 0.46% buyer lifetime savings and a 17.85% seller
equity IRR, but minimum DSCR was only 1.143x, below the modeled lender gate.
Lowering price helped the buyer, but weakened financing.

**Audience prompt:** "If price cannot pass all gates, what structural lever
changes the financing?"

**Key caveat:** Do not say all Vietnam DPPAs are unbankable. Say that pricing
alone did not balance modeled Case 6 under the tested assumptions.

**Transition:** Translate the result into realistic buyer expectations.

---

## Slide 23 - The practical buyer expectation is value under constraints, not automatic savings

**Objective:** Convert the case-study evidence into procurement posture.

**Detailed narrative:**

Explain the three practical expectations. First, Year 1 can be difficult
because the offered strike and other DPPA costs may begin above BAU. Second,
the evaluation horizon matters; buyers should examine Year 1, 10-year
cumulative, and lifetime results rather than selecting only the most favorable
period. Third, financing structure can determine whether a buyer-positive price
is feasible.

If price changes alone cannot satisfy all gates, the parties may need to
consider project sizing, leverage, debt sculpting, reserves, tenor, guarantees,
or different risk allocation. The correct buyer expectation is disciplined
value under real constraints, not automatic savings.

**Buyer action:** Prepare internal approval around long-term cost, renewable
claims, and risk allocation.

**Transition:** Close with the five questions that should change the term
sheet.

---

## Slide 24 - Leave the session with five questions that change the term sheet

**Objective:** Convert learning into a negotiation checklist.

**Detailed narrative:**

Review the five questions as the buyer's closing checklist. First, what is the
strike level and how is it indexed or escalated? Second, how is hourly contract
volume determined and reset? Third, what are the tenor, termination, and
load-change protections? Fourth, which voltage and loss-factor assumptions
settle the bill? Fifth, how are market, regulatory, volume, financing, and
attribute risks allocated?

Explain that these questions should produce changes to data requirements,
modeling assumptions, and contract language. They are not only discussion
questions.

Close by connecting the lender-gate issue to the afternoon risk discussion:
when buyer-positive pricing causes DSCR to fail, financing structure becomes a
commercial negotiation topic.

**Closing message:** Ask for evidence, test all three gates, and negotiate the
structure rather than accepting the headline price.

## Slide 25 - Use the formula sheet to separate regulated settlement logic from training-model inputs

**Use when:** Participants request the settlement algebra or challenge the fee
assumptions.

**Detailed narrative:**

Explain each item by its commercial purpose. Q_adj adjusts generator output for
loss treatment. Q_Khc caps buyer settlement based on matched consumption.
C_DN represents renewable-energy payment through EVN. C_DPPA and C_CL add
service and balancing components. The CfD creates the bilateral
strike-minus-market settlement.

Then separate the structure from the numerical inputs. The mechanism and
quantity relationships may be grounded in regulation and the training

values are training or model assumptions where current numerical

**Buyer diligence point:** Confirm every formula input's source, effective

## Slide 26 - FMP and CFMP uncertainty should be tested as scenarios, not treated as a point forecast

**Use when:** Participants ask which future market-price forecast should be
used.

**Detailed narrative:**

Explain that the buyer should not depend on one precise FMP or CFMP forecast.
In a low-market-price case, the strike may exceed market price and create a
larger buyer CfD payment. In a high-market-price case, the CfD may generate a
payment to the buyer, but basis and volume mismatch can still limit the hedge.
The base case should reflect the negotiated term sheet and a documented market
assumption, not an unsupported claim of certainty.

Recommend at least low, base, and high market-price paths, combined with tariff
and volume scenarios. Report results at Year 1, 10-year cumulative, and
lifetime horizons.

**Key caveat:** No official forward FMP forecast was used in this deck.

## Slide 27 - A production slowdown turns volume language into the buyer's first risk question

**Use when:** Participants are concerned about production volatility, customer
orders, shutdowns, or facility relocation.

**Detailed narrative:**

Explain the downside-load sequence. If factory load falls, metered consumption
may become lower than the expected contracted shape. The settlement cap may
reduce the useful CfD quantity because settlement is tested against matched
load and adjusted renewable volume. The remaining exposure then depends on
negotiated provisions such as take-or-pay, volume-reset rights, termination,
and responsibility for unmatched generation.

The buyer should request a downside-load table before accepting annual volume
commitments. Test at least 70%, 50%, and temporary-shutdown conditions.

**Audience prompt:** "What happens if the contracted facility runs below plan
or shuts for a month?"

**Key emphasis:** Production risk becomes contract risk when volume language is
inflexible.

## Slide 28 - Treat Scope 2 and REC language as an evidence checklist, not an assumption

**Use when:** Participants ask whether signing the DPPA automatically supports
Scope 2 claims.

**Detailed narrative:**

Explain that the commercial contract should clearly address environmental
attributes. The buyer should identify who owns the attributes or certificates,
what tracking evidence demonstrates generation and retirement, what allocation
evidence connects the attributes to the buyer, and what exact claim language
can be supported for brands, auditors, and customers.

The contract should also define remedies if certificates, meter data,
retirement evidence, or delivery documentation are missing. This turns a
sustainability intention into an auditable evidence process.

**Key caveat:** This deck does not assert that a Vietnam DPPA automatically
qualifies for a specific Scope 2 claim. No external Scope 2 standard source was
added to the deck.

## Slide 29 - Eligibility and loss factors are small-looking inputs with direct price consequences

**Use when:** Participants need more detail on voltage qualification or Kpp.

**Detailed narrative:**

Reinforce that 22 kV or above is the national-grid DPPA buyer eligibility gate
identified in Decree 57. Then explain that Kpp affects delivered settlement
volume and therefore directly affects cost and value. Because Kpp is calculated
and updated, the buyer should understand which value applies, how it is
published, and how annual changes flow through the contract.

The Factory A value of 1.027263 is a model assumption informed by training
context. The 1.008 value is a 110 kV training example. Neither is presented as
a confirmed current official Kpp.

**Buyer action:** Require documentation of voltage class, Kpp basis, effective
date, and sensitivity to annual updates.

## Slide 30 - The buyer gate is not the seller gate, so the model reports them separately

**Use when:** Participants challenge why a project can be attractive to the
seller but unattractive to the buyer.

**Detailed narrative:**

Explain the measurement logic. The buyer gate compares DPPA cost with BAU and
looks for acceptable value across relevant horizons. The seller gate evaluates
equity IRR against the investor's hurdle. The lender gate evaluates minimum
DSCR against the financing floor. A balanced deal requires all gates to pass
together.

Use Case 6 at 2,000 VND/kWh as the example: buyer savings are negative 14.4%,
seller equity IRR is positive 26.9%, and minimum DSCR is 1.50x. Across the 56
tested Case 6 scenarios, no scenario passed all gates together.

**Model-limit reminder:** Factory A uses fixed FX, modeled Kpp and fee
assumptions, and tested strike-volume scenarios. It is a negotiation screen,
not a market forecast.
